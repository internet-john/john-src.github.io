Tips for Web Dev Students

A lot of students have asked me what their portfolio should look like to come to graduation. Should they have one already? What makes a good portfolio project? How do they make themselves a strong candidate for a specific company?

Some thoughts

Your coursework now should be added to your portfolio on GitHub. All of it. We all had to start somewhere, so do not be shy to upload even the most basic things. What matters is your growth trajectory. As an employer, I want to see that growth -- from learning basic data and decision structures up to your capstone project.

One of the professional software engineer's greatest strength lies in their ability to learn quickly, and translate that knowledge into a working solution. As a junior engineer, you're literally being paid to learn.

Project Ideas What do YOU enjoy? What could you talk about for hours and hours endlessly and drive your friends and family crazy? What problems do YOU want to solve? What products do YOU enjoy?

Perhaps you love Netflix! Netflix has very specific engineering challenges they grapple with that are different from other companies. Go deep on streaming technologies, distributed systems, relational databases.

Use https://stackshare.io/ to find a company's tech stack.

It's very important to identify your passions and what you want to be building and solving for as early as possible. The sheer amount of knowledge and opportunities are incredible, but also overwhelming! Make it easier and more enjoyable by focusing on what gets you excited, and you will do exceptional work.

With this being said, there is absolutely a baseline of fundamentals that all software engineers should be familiar with and able to implement.

I recommend Freecodecamp.com projects

Do all of them! Do all of them again, but in a different language! Can you figure out how to deploy a node service to AWS, expose a public API, and consume that in your web app? There are countless ways to solve the problem. Keep things spicy and challenge yourself to do it differently each time!

Deliberate, consistent, practice.

Reading about algorithms and documentation can be dry at times. However, running into the problem yourself on a project, finding a solution, and discovering the raison d'être is the optimal learning experience. In my opinion :) I've been coding professionally for 4 years and still do these freecodecamp projects to keep my skills sharp.

As for documentation, I recommend Chrome web dev documentation and Mozilla web dev documentation

TL;DR

Expose your ignorance. Showcase and be proud of your growth. Build a strong foundation. Build a portfolio that is geared towards companies you want to work for / problems you want to be solving. Consistent, deliberate, practice.

Enough reading. Go build!
